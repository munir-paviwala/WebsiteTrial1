Abstract

This website-essay will explore the various theories under the roof called 'new materialism', and then try to understand how these might affect the ways in which we study environmental history. To understand how such histories are possible, this online project will explore the case of two of the major works that have taken steps in such a direction. It ends by highlighting some of the limitations of such narratives, and with a note on the future direction of research.

What are the theories under the broad title of 'New Materialism'?

Have you ever considered how the objects around you affect your life and the world? If so, you might be interested in learning about New Materialism.

Broadly, New Materialism is a theoretical framework that emphasizes the active role of material things and the material world in shaping human experiences and social structures.

It challenges traditional dualisms between the human and non-human, mind and body, and nature and culture.

Now, what does that mean?

In a traditional dualistic view:

Human vs Non-Human

The user (human) is often considered separate from the smartphone (non-human) they use.

Nature vs Culture

The smartphone is typically categorized as a product of technology, distinct from the natural world.

Now, let's apply New Materialism theories:

Entangled Relationships

In a New Materialist perspective, the user and the smartphone are not separate entities but are entangled in a complex relationship. The smartphone is not just a passive tool; it actively shapes and influences the user's behavior and experiences.

Nature-Culture Continuum

Instead of seeing the smartphone as purely a cultural artifact, New Materialism invites us to consider the natural resources, energy, and environmental impact involved in producing and maintaining the device. This blurs the boundaries between nature and culture, highlighting the ecological footprint of technological objects.

Affect and Assemblages

"DeleuzoGuattarian materialism regards human bodies and all other material, social and abstract entities as relational, having no ontological status or integrity until drawn into 'assemblages' with other similarly contingent and ephemeral bodies, things and ideas through their capacities to affect or be affected" (Fox & Alldred, 2019).

Common Themes

Critique of Traditional Dualisms

All four scholars challenge traditional dualisms such as nature/culture, human/non-human, and mind/body.

Relational Ontology

Objects and entities gain meaning through their relationships and interactions with other entities.

Materiality as Active

Matter is lively, intelligent and self-organizing, playing an active role in shaping our realities.

Applications in Environmental History

Thinking Like a Mountain (1949)

Aldo Leopold's groundbreaking essay that attempts to imagine the world from the perspective of a mountain, exploring how the mountain would have perceived the world around it.

"Only the mountain has lived long enough to listen objectively to the howl of a wolf."

Leopold strives to transcend the human perspective and comprehend the environment beyond human time scales, delving into geological time scales that span centuries and millennia.

Future Directions

New materialism offers a promising shift in perspective, challenging us to reconsider human-centric narratives in environmental history. These theories provide frameworks for understanding the past without putting humans at the center of everything.

It is a difficult task, but perhaps an important one for our current climate crisis to understand that it is not only about us.

By exploring these theories, we can develop new ways of understanding the complex relationships between humans and non-humans throughout history.
