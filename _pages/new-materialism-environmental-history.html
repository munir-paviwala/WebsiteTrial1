New Materialism & Environmental History

"Until the lions have their own historians, the history of the hunt will always glorify the hunter"
- African Proverb

Introduction

In this project, I highlight and explore the theories which are often called together as 'new materialism'. Following that, I highlight how this theory can help shape a new kind of environmental history - a way of history which de-centers the humans from the narratives of environmental history.

I am arguing that new materialism offers a different nuanced and complex understanding of the interaction between the humans and non-humans in the past.

This website-essay explores various theories under the roof called 'new materialism', and how these might affect the ways we study environmental history.

Common Themes

Critique of Traditional Dualisms

All four scholars challenge traditional dualisms such as nature/culture, human/non-human, and mind/body.

Relational Ontology

Objects and entities gain meaning through their relationships and interactions with other entities.

Materiality as Active

Matter is lively, intelligent and self-organizing, playing an active role in shaping our realities.

Applications in Environmental History

Thinking Like a Mountain (1949)

Aldo Leopold's groundbreaking essay that attempts to imagine the world from the perspective of a mountain, exploring how the mountain would have perceived the world around it.

"Only the mountain has lived long enough to listen objectively to the howl of a wolf."

Leopold strives to transcend the human perspective and comprehend the environment beyond human time scales, delving into geological time scales that span centuries and millennia.

Mountain landscape representing geological time scales
Aldo Leopold, environmental philosopher

Future Directions

New materialism offers a promising shift in perspective, challenging us to reconsider human-centric narratives in environmental history. These theories provide frameworks for understanding the past without putting humans at the center of everything.

It is a difficult task, but perhaps an important one for our current climate crisis to understand that it is not only about us.

By exploring these theories, we can develop new ways of understanding the complex relationships between humans and non-humans throughout history.
